The story

Why players do
what they do.

I've spent fifteen years asking that one question from every seat a game studio has. It turns out that question is the whole job.

I started in QA, breaking games on purpose. I was the Lead Online QA Analyst at Sony, testing a long run of PlayStation launches, then a Senior QA Analyst at EA on the Pogo.com titles. That work taught me to look for the edge cases: if something can break, some player will find the way. (I like the symmetry that I started at Sony and, fifteen years later, I'm back at PlayStation.)

Next I moved into the game itself as a designer, building quests, dungeons, rewards, and the first hour of play for an online RPG. That taught me the thing I've never forgotten: every number on a dashboard is a real person deciding whether your game is worth their evening. Pacing, friction, and clarity aren't abstract. They decide who stays and who quietly leaves.

Then I went somewhere most product people never do: the security side. At Zynga I worked on payment risk and cheating across some of the biggest social games of the era. At ArenaNet I led fraud and game security for Guild Wars 2, protecting one of the most loved economies in the genre. I learned to think like the person trying to break the game, and to weigh every fix against its cost to honest players.

Security in games isn't really a tech problem. It's a trust problem. And trust is one of the most valuable things a live game owns.

From there I moved from protecting the game to steering it. As Analytics Lead and PM on Guild Wars 2, I owned the health metrics and shipped live events tuned from how players actually behaved. I founded the analytics team at Gearbox, standardized analytics across live teams at SciPlay, and built product, design, and analytics from scratch at HiDef for a new live-service game.

More recently I worked inside AAA teams at ZeniMax as Lead PM for Insights & Product, and today I lead telemetry work at PlayStation, making sure game teams across the platform can measure their players and act on what they see.

QA taught me to find the cracks. Design taught me empathy. Security taught me to stay skeptical. Analytics taught me to prove things. Product is where all of it works at once.

What I do best is sit in the middle of a studio and translate. I can talk pacing with designers, numbers with analysts, trade-offs with engineers, and roadmap and business health with leadership, and keep every one of those conversations pointed at the same player. That's the work I want: close to games, close to players, turning messy questions into decisions a team can rally behind.
