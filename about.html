The story

From breaking games
to building teams.

I've worked in games for almost twenty years, and I've come at them from nearly every angle: testing them, designing them, defending them, measuring them, and finally leading the product and analytics work behind them.

I started in QA, breaking games on purpose. At Sony I was a lead QA analyst for the launch of the PlayStation 3, testing online titles before they went live. Later I did QA at EA. The job was simple to describe and hard to do well: find the ways a game falls apart before players do. That mindset, looking for the seam everyone else missed, stuck with me for good.

Then I crossed over to making games. I was a designer on Earth Eternal, an MMORPG, where I built quests, dungeons, skills, rewards, and the first hour of play. Designing the start of an online RPG is a lesson in how fragile early engagement is: a confusing first hour loses people who would have loved the game an hour later. I learned to care about pacing, clarity, and reward, and to sweat the experience from the player's side of the screen.

From there I spent years on the security and integrity side. At Zynga I worked on payment risk and cheating across some of the biggest social games of the era. At ArenaNet I led fraud and game security for Guild Wars 2, protecting one of the most loved economies in the genre. I wasn't doing product work yet, I was the person thinking like an attacker, finding exploits, and weighing every fix against its cost to honest players.

Security in games isn't really a tech problem. It's a trust problem. And trust is one of the most valuable things a live game owns.

That security work is also where I started living in player data, because catching abuse means reading behavior at scale. It pulled me toward analytics, and at ArenaNet I moved into a new role: Analytics Lead and Product Manager on Guild Wars 2. For the first time the question wasn't “how is this being broken?” but “how is this game actually doing, and what do we change next?” I owned the health metrics and shipped live events tuned from how players really behaved.

After that, the work became about building the function, not just doing it. I founded the analytics practice at Gearbox, standardized analytics across live teams at SciPlay, and at HiDef built product, design, and analytics from scratch for a new live-service game. A lot of that was mentoring, growing people into the roles a maturing live game needs and setting up the rituals a team runs on.

More recently I was Lead PM for Insights & Product at ZeniMax, embedded with AAA teams, and today I lead telemetry and streaming data work at PlayStation, spanning the platform, its devices, and the wider ecosystem, so teams across the company can measure how players and systems behave and act on what they see.

Each seat taught me something the next one needed. QA taught me to find the cracks. Design taught me to respect the player's time. Security taught me to stay skeptical. Analytics taught me to prove things instead of arguing them. Leading product and teams is where all of it finally works together.

Two things I keep coming back to. First, live service is its own craft: launching a game into it and maturing a game already in it are different problems, and I've done both. Second, the work that lasts is the team you leave behind. Whether a studio is standing up its first analytics function or leveling up an established product group, I build and mentor the people and set up the habits so the game keeps improving after I've moved on.

What I do best is sit in the middle of a studio and connect the dots. I can talk pacing with designers, numbers with analysts, trade-offs with engineers, and roadmap and business health with leadership, and keep all of those conversations pointed at the same player. That's the work I want: close to games, close to players, building teams that turn hard questions into decisions everyone can get behind.
